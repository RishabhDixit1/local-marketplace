## 1. The Problem We Are Solving

### The Gap Nobody Has Fixed

Every day, in every colony, gully, and neighbourhood across urban and semi-urban India, two things happen simultaneously and never meet:

1. **Someone has a small problem they cannot solve alone.** A fan stops working. A parcel needs to go across the street. An elderly parent needs someone to stand in a bank queue. A student needs a document printed at 11 PM.

2. **Someone nearby has the skill, tool, or free time to solve it** — a shopkeeper waiting for customers, a college student between classes, a retiree with nothing to do at 2 PM, a freelance electrician with no leads today.

These two people are often within 500 metres of each other. They never connect.

**The existing solutions fail this gap entirely:**

| Platform | Why it fails micro-needs |
|---|---|
| Urban Company / Sulekha | Minimum ticket size, formal onboarding, city-scale only, not real-time |
| WhatsApp groups | Passive, no discovery, no accountability, no payment, no structure |
| Contacts and neighbours | Works only if you know the right person, breaks for strangers |
| Swiggy Genie / Porter | Delivery-only, not skill-based, not conversational |
| OLX / Quikr | Ad listing platform, not a task-completion engine |

None of them handle the most common category of human need: **unstructured, low-value, hyperlocal, time-sensitive tasks** where the value is not in the product but in the *willingness to help right now*.

### Organic Validation — Reddit r/founder (March 2026)

A post by u/anythingdone on r/founder titled **"Very common ignored problem — No solution, pls suggest if I should work on it?"** articulates the exact same friction this platform is built to eliminate. The post was shared independently of ServiQ and reached the same conclusions through lived experience:

> *"There are so many small situations where you need quick help — like fixing something, assembling furniture, carrying items, or getting something locally."*

The four real-life situations described in the post map directly to categories ServiQ handles:

| Their situation | What they said | ServiQ category |
|---|---|---|
| Situation 1 | Light kharab hai, electrician ka number nahi — *Kash koi nearby mil jaye* | Home & Repair #2 — Light switch repair |
| Situation 3 | Ghar me koi nahi, cylinder uthana hai — *Koi help mil jaye 10 min ke liye* | Quick Personal Help #21 — Carry heavy saamaan |
| Situation 4 | Late night charger chahiye — *Kisi ke paas hoga nearby* | Local / Urgent Needs #44 — Arrange charger/adapter |

The post's diagnosis:

> *"Log bade problems ke liye apps use karte hain. Par chhoti problems ke liye struggle karte hain."*  
> *(People use apps for big problems. But for small problems, they struggle.)*

> *"These are too small for service apps, and usually we depend on: neighbours, contacts, random jugaad."*

And the solution the post converges on:

> *"At the same time, there are people nearby: with skills, with free time, who could help and even earn."*  
> *"Post any small need → Nearby people or shopkeepers respond → Get it done quickly."*

This is word-for-word the ServiQ value proposition, arrived at independently by a founder on Reddit. **It confirms the problem is real, widely felt, and not yet solved by anything currently in the market.** The fact that someone else is thinking about it validates urgency — but the fact that ServiQ already has the realtime infrastructure, trust layer, and geo-matching built means execution, not idea, is now the differentiator.

## 2. The Core Insight

> Small problems are everywhere.  
> Solutions are also everywhere.  
> There is no system connecting them **instantly**.

This is not a market gap in the traditional sense. It is a **coordination failure**. Supply and demand exist. The infrastructure to match them in real time, with trust and payment, does not.

ServiQ is that infrastructure.

### What makes this different from any service marketplace:

- **No category restriction.** Any task a human can perform for another human is valid.
- **No minimum ticket size.** A ₹30 print job matters as much as a ₹3,000 repair.
- **Real-time matching.** Not a listing you post and wait 2 days for. A need expressed now, matched now.
- **Zero formal onboarding for helpers.** Anyone with time and willingness can earn. Not just registered "vendors".
- **Hyper-local radius.** The entire experience is scoped to walking or two-minute travel distance.

- Urban households with two working adults who cannot spare time for micro-errands
- Students needing last-minute academic help or printing
- Elderly residents who need help with phone apps, bank forms, medicine pickup
- Anyone who has a task that is too small to call a professional but too inconvenient to ignore

### Supply side (Helpers / Providers)
- Local shopkeepers with slow hours who want walk-in or delivery leads
- Freelance tradespeople (electricians, plumbers, carpenters) who lack digital discovery
- College students wanting to monetise free time between classes
- Retirees or homemakers with skills and availability
- Gig-ready individuals looking for supplemental income without formal employment

**The supply side is the most underutilised economic asset in India.** Millions of people have time and skills that evaporate every day without creating any value because there was no platform to surface that availability to a nearby person with a matching need.

## 5. Revenue Model

### Unit Economics

| Event | Fee |
|---|---|
| Successful task match and completion | ₹40–50 per transaction |
| Premium provider visibility (subscription) | ₹299–999/month |
| AI Launchpad business setup | ₹499 one-time or bundled |
| Urgent/priority task boost | ₹20–30 per post |

### City-Scale Projections

**Single city (e.g. Noida):**
- 5,000 daily posts × 30% conversion = 1,500 completed tasks/day
- 1,500 × ₹40 = **₹60,000/day → ₹18L/month**

**Aggressive scale (1 city, high adoption):**
- 10,000 tasks/day × ₹50 = ₹5,00,000/day
- **₹1.5 Cr/month per city**

**10-city expansion:**
- ₹15 Cr/month → **₹180 Cr+ annually**

The model is capital-light and scales linearly with supply-demand density. Each new neighbourhood that reaches critical mass compounds network effects.

## 6. Technical Approach

ServiQ is built as a **real-time local services operating system**, not a static listing directory. Every technical decision maps back to a specific product requirement from the problem statement above.

### 6.1 Technology Stack

| Layer | Technology | Why this choice |
|---|---|---|
| Frontend framework | Next.js 16 (App Router) + React 19 | SSR for public pages (SEO, business profiles), client-side reactivity for realtime UX |
| Language | TypeScript | End-to-end type safety across API contracts and UI state |
| Backend / Database | Supabase (Postgres + Auth + Realtime + RLS) | Real-time subscriptions, row-level security for multi-tenant data isolation, no separate backend infra |
| Styling | Tailwind CSS 4 | Rapid iteration on dense information-rich layouts |
| Maps | MapLibre GL (OpenFreeMap tiles) | Open-source, no per-tile billing, offline-capable for future, geo polygon support |
| Realtime | Supabase Channels (WebSocket) | Presence, PostgreSQL change events, typing indicators — all over one persistent connection |
| Animations | Framer Motion | Card entrance, toast, modal transitions without layout jank |
| Forms | react-hook-form | Validated, accessible input handling for posting needs and provider onboarding |
| Deployment | Vercel | Edge CDN, zero-config Next.js, preview environments per PR |
| Auth | Supabase Auth + Exotel OTP (India-primary) | Phone number as identity — no email required, lower friction for semi-urban users |
| Testing | Vitest (unit) + Playwright (e2e) | Fast logic tests + authenticated smoke tests on critical paths |

### 6.2 How Realtime Matching Works

The coordination failure described in Section 2 is solved architecturally through Supabase Realtime:

```
Seeker posts a need
        ↓
INSERT → help_requests table (Postgres)
        ↓
Supabase broadcasts postgres_changes event to all subscribed clients
        ↓
Nearby providers receive the event within ~200ms
        ↓
Provider responds → triggers task/order creation
        ↓
Both parties enter a shared Deal Room (chat + quote + files)
```

Every participant on the People and Welcome feed pages holds an active WebSocket subscription scoped to their user ID. This means a provider does not need to refresh or poll — new demand appears in their feed instantly.

The `provider_presence` table (upserted every 60 seconds per active session) allows the platform to know which providers are *online right now*, enabling the UI to surface "Active nearby" as a trust signal and to prioritise routing new requests to available helpers.

### 6.3 Geo-Based Discovery Architecture

Location is the product's core discriminator. The technical implementation:

- **Browser Geolocation API** fetches precise coordinates on page load (with a 4-second timeout fallback).
- If the browser denies location, the system falls back to `resolveCoordinates()` — a deterministic function that extracts lat/lng from profile data or seeds a consistent jittered coordinate from the user's ID. **No user is ever shown a blank map.**
- **`distanceBetweenCoordinatesKm()`** computes Haversine distance client-side for all visible providers, used in rank scoring.
- **`calculateLocalRankScore()`** combines distance, response time, rating, and profile completeness into a single 1–100 rank score per provider card. Online providers receive a +4 boost, creating a live incentive to stay active.
- MapLibre GL renders all visible providers as map pins, with the active card highlighted in sync with the scroll position of the provider list — a linked map-list interface that makes spatial density visible at a glance.

### 6.4 Trust Architecture

For the micro-task market to work, strangers must trust each other quickly. The trust system is layered:

| Layer | Implementation |
|---|---|
| Identity | Supabase Auth with phone OTP (Exotel); email fallback |
| Profile completeness | `calculateProfileCompletion()` scores 7 profile fields; shown as % to the viewer and factored into rank |
| Verification badge | `calculateVerificationStatus()` aggregates role, completion %, listing count, rating, and review count into a `verified` / `trusted` / `new` status |
| Social proof | Review aggregation in `ratingMap`; review count and average surfaced on every card |
| Presence signal | `provider_presence.is_online`, `availability`, `rolling_response_minutes` — real behavioural signals, not self-declared |
| Completed jobs | `provider_order_stats` table; `completedJobs` and `openLeads` shown on provider cards |
| Trust blurb | Dynamically generated text per provider based on their actual data tier |

### 6.5 Data Model — Core Tables

```
profiles              — identity, role, location, bio, completion %
help_requests         — demand-side needs (category, description, budget, location)
service_listings      — supply-side offerings (provider, category, price, description)
product_catalog       — physical product supply
posts                 — general community activity (demand / supply / discussion)
orders / tasks        — lifecycle: pending → active → in-progress → completed / cancelled
connection_requests   — social graph (requester, recipient, status, timestamps)
feed_card_saves       — bookmarked provider cards or marketplace items
reviews               — rating + text, linked to provider_id
provider_presence     — is_online, availability, response SLAs, last_seen
provider_order_stats  — completed_jobs, open_leads per provider
```

Row-Level Security (RLS) policies are enforced at the database layer, meaning even if an API route has a bug, a user cannot read or write another user's private data. This is critical for a platform where strangers transact.

### 6.6 The Feed as a Matching Engine

The `buildCommunityFeedView()` server function is the heart of demand-supply matching. It assembles a unified feed from:
- `service_listings` (supply)
- `product_catalog` (supply)
- `posts` (demand + community)
- `help_requests` (demand)

Each item is tagged with its type, enriched with provider metadata, and sorted by recency and relevance. Seekers see supply. Providers see demand. The same feed serves both roles, creating a natural bidirectional discovery surface.

### 6.7 AI Launchpad — Accelerating Supply Onboarding

The bottleneck for any marketplace is supply onboarding. ServiQ's AI Launchpad addresses this directly:

- A structured 7–10 question onboarding flow collects business type, services, pricing model, service radius, hours, and brand tone.
- The AI layer generates storefront copy, service listings, pricing packs, and FAQ content automatically.
- A business that previously needed a web developer and hours of work can go live in under 5 minutes.
- This compresses the time-to-first-lead from days to minutes, directly reducing supply-side churn.

### 6.8 Quote and Deal Flow

Once a match is made, the platform keeps both parties inside a structured workflow:

```
Inbound need → Lead OS (qualify, score, route)
                      ↓
             Draft first response
                      ↓
             Quote with line items, revisions
                      ↓
             Files, proof-of-work, photos
                      ↓
             Approval → task marked complete
                      ↓
             Payment state + invoice
                      ↓
             Review request triggered
```

This replaces the current reality where a seeker calls a plumber, there is no written scope, the price is disputed, and neither party has any record. The Deal Room is the accountability layer the unorganised micro-task economy has always lacked.

## 7. Why This Works Now

### Supply-side enablers

- **Smartphone penetration in Tier 2/3 India has crossed 60%** — the supply side can now be found on a mobile app.
- **UPI adoption** means instant payment settlement is already a habit. Zero friction to pay ₹50 for a task.
- **Gig economy normalisation** — post-COVID, supplemental income from short tasks is socially acceptable and actively sought.

### Demand-side enablers

- **Nuclear family urbanisation** — no extended family nearby to help with small tasks. The "neighbour who helps" social layer is eroding in cities.
- **Time poverty** — dual-income households have money but not time for errands.
- **Trust deficit in unorganised sector** — people want verified local help, not random numbers from a WhatsApp group.

### Platform timing

- No dominant player has locked in the **sub-₹500, sub-30-minute, hyperlocal task** category.
- The realtime infrastructure (Supabase Realtime at scale, WebSocket-native mobile) only became cheap and reliable enough to build on in the last 2–3 years.
- AI-generated onboarding content makes supply-side activation fast enough to bootstrap new cities without a sales team.

## 11. Core Strategic Thesis — The AI-Native Local Commerce Engine

*Source: Core_Thesis.pdf — internal strategic document*

> *"If this becomes valuable, it will not be because it is 'an AI marketplace.' It will be valuable because it becomes the operating system for messy, unstructured local demand."*

### Why India Now

Local commerce in India is still broken in predictable, structural ways:

- Demand is fragmented across calls, WhatsApp, search, referrals, and neighbourhood groups
- Supply is informal, inconsistent, and poorly digitized
- Trust is weak between strangers
- Intent is usually conversational, not catalog-based
- Fulfillment often needs back-and-forth before money changes hands

That fragmentation is the opening. **The product should not look like a directory with AI added on top. It should act like an AI local commerce engine that takes raw human intent and converts it into trusted local transactions.**

### What Is Unique — Demand-First, Not Listing-First

Most local platforms start with inventory:

- search businesses
- compare providers
- call or message manually
- negotiate manually
- trust manually

ServiQ's approach starts with the need:

- user states a need in natural language, voice, photo, or chat
- AI converts that into structured intent
- system identifies urgency, location, budget, category, risk, and fulfillment type
- engine routes it to the right local providers
- AI helps both sides clarify scope, timing, and price
- platform captures trust and transaction outcomes
- engine improves over time

This is an **intent-to-fulfillment engine**, not a discovery layer.

### The 4 Product Layers

**1. Intent Layer**

User expresses a need:
- "Need AC service today in Dwarka under 1500"
- "Need someone to fix a leaking tap near me"
- "Can anyone deliver school books by evening?"

AI extracts: category, urgency, locality, budget, preferred time, trust sensitivity, likely supply type, and missing details — then asks only the minimum follow-up questions.

**2. Trust Layer**

Local commerce fails mainly on trust. Ranking cannot be simple distance + price. A trust score must be built from: response speed, completion rate, repeat usage, dispute rate, cancellation rate, review quality, identity strength, local proximity, and service-category consistency. **This becomes one of the strongest moats if captured well.**

**3. Provider OS**

Providers do not need another passive listing app. They need an earning engine. Provider-side AI should help with: lead qualification, suggested replies, quote generation, schedule management, customer follow-ups, upsell/cross-sell suggestions, missed lead recovery, and profile improvement tips. If providers earn more with less effort, supply retention becomes much stronger.

**4. Transaction Layer**

The engine must eventually manage: discovery → matching → chat → quoting → booking → payment → proof of work → review → repeat. Without this layer, the system becomes another lead generator, which is the weaker business.

### India-First Advantages

India gives unusually strong conditions for this model:
- Huge informal supply base
- WhatsApp-native conversational behaviour
- Multilingual demand
- Low trust in unknown providers → high value of a trust layer
- High sensitivity to convenience and price
- Local commerce often begins as conversation, not search

The India-first edge should be expressed as: voice and multilingual input, locality-level matching, high-trust provider surfacing, lightweight provider onboarding, chat-first negotiation, mobile-first workflow.

**Position it as: the first AI-native commerce engine built for how local transactions actually happen — not "India version of X."**

### Ethical AI as a Concrete Differentiator

"Ethical AI" must be operational, not branding:

- Transparent ranking factors (providers know why they rank where they do)
- Clear AI-vs-human boundaries
- No fake provider inflation or liquidity staging
- No manipulation of urgency or pricing
- Provider fairness controls
- Customer privacy by default
- Explicit consent for profile and chat usage
- Explainable recommendations
- Human override for disputes and safety cases

This matters especially in local commerce, where provider livelihoods are directly affected by platform decisions.

### MVP Recommendation

Do not start with "everything local." Start with one wedge where:
- need is frequent
- intent is messy enough for AI to add value
- trust matters
- fulfillment can be closed in-platform

**Recommended wedge:** urgent and semi-urgent home services + neighbourhood needs — electrician, plumber, AC repair, appliance fix, quick local tasks.

Why: clear pain, high trust requirement, repeat frequency, strong local radius, conversation-heavy demand, easier monetisation than generic classifieds.

**MVP shape:** 1 city or dense micro-market, 2–3 categories, one provider type cluster, strong repeat loop.

**MVP user flow:**

For seekers:
1. Describe need in text / voice / photo
2. AI structures request
3. User confirms scope
4. Engine matches 3–5 best providers
5. AI-assisted chat and quote
6. Booking or acceptance
7. Completion and review

For providers:
1. Receive structured lead
2. AI suggests response / quote
3. Accept or decline fast
4. Complete job
5. Capture proof and feedback
6. Improve ranking

**AI in MVP — where it creates measurable lift:**
- Intent parsing and auto-category mapping
- Missing info prompts
- Lead prioritisation
- Smart provider matching
- AI reply suggestions
- Quote assistance
- Trust / risk flags
- Spam / fraud detection

**Avoid in MVP:** generic AI chatbot everywhere, over-automated pricing, fake conversational layers with no operational value, broad recommendation systems before data exists.

### The 3 Reinforcing Growth Loops

**1. Demand loop**
Better intent capture → better matches → better outcomes → more repeat demand

**2. Supply loop**
Better leads → better conversion → more provider earnings → better supply retention

**3. Data loop**
More interactions → better trust and matching models → better marketplace efficiency

These three loops compound each other. Each transaction improves the engine that produces the next transaction.

### What Becomes the Real Moat

The moat is not AI. AI alone is not defensible. The moat is the combination of:

- **Local intent graph** — who asks for what, when, where, at what urgency, at what price
- **Provider behavior data** — response speed, completion rates, category consistency
- **Fulfillment outcome data** — what actually got done vs. what was requested
- **Trust graph** — provider reliability relative to category and location
- **Category-specific workflows** — conversation patterns that close jobs, not just leads
- **Conversational transaction data** — AI tuned on actual local transaction outcomes, not generic internet text

That graph is the real asset. A new entrant cannot replicate it — they must earn it one transaction at a time.

### Key Risks to Manage

- Too many categories too early — dilutes depth before liquidity exists
- Fake liquidity — undermines trust at the exact moment trust is being built
- Long time-to-fulfillment — users fall back to WhatsApp if the platform is slower
- AI hallucinating provider fit or pricing — harms both sides of the market
- Trying to scale before one local cluster works deeply

**The principle: depth before breadth.**

Investor-grade version:

> *"We are building the trust and fulfillment layer for local commerce, starting in India, where most local demand is still unstructured and underserved."*
